# Fake Pompeii Facts — English Reference

For quick reference or if Javier needs to improvise with English-speaking tourists.

---

## LEVEL 0: Almost True (real facts with one small false twist)

_Real Pompeii facts with ONE detail changed. Very hard to catch._

1. **Stepping stones** — The raised stones were for crossing streets above sewage
   runoff. Their height was legally standardized at 32cm to match military cart axle
   clearance. _[True: stones existed for that purpose. False: no standardized 32cm
   legal measurement — heights varied by street.]_

2. **The Lupanar frescos** — The erotic frescos above each door were a visual menu
   for foreign sailors who didn't speak Latin. Each fresco had a different price carved
   below it in Roman numerals. _[True: the brothel and frescos are real. False: prices
   weren't carved under each individual fresco.]_

3. **Thermopolia food** — These fast-food counters served hot food in embedded vessels.
   Remains of lentils, pork, and even giraffe were found in one — Romans ate exotic
   meat as delicacies. _[True: thermopolia and food remains are real. False: giraffe
   was found in Herculaneum in a different context, not in a Pompeii thermopolium.]_

4. **Oldest amphitheatre** — Pompeii's amphitheatre (c. 70 BC) predates the Colosseum
   by 150 years. After a massive brawl between Pompeiians and Nucerians in 59 AD, the
   Roman Senate banned games there for ten full years. _[True: the amphitheatre and the
   riot are real (Tacitus documented it). False: the ban was lifted early by Nero — the
   ten years weren't fully served.]_

5. **Plaster casts** — When ash-buried bodies decomposed, they left hollow cavities.
   In 1863, Giuseppe Fiorelli filled them with plaster, creating these casts. Over
   1,100 have been made. _[True: Fiorelli's technique is real. False: about 100 casts
   were made, not 1,100.]_

6. **Garden of the Fugitives** — 13 people, including children, died fleeing through
   the Nocera Gate. DNA analysis showed they were three unrelated families who grouped
   together by survival instinct. _[True: the Garden and 13 victims are real. False:
   the DNA "three unrelated families" narrative is fabricated.]_

7. **Via dell'Abbondanza** — The main commercial street. Cart ruts are visible in the
   stones. Traffic was so heavy there were one-way streets. Pompeii was the first city
   with pedestrian-only streets — some had stone bollards blocking carts. _[True: the
   street and cart ruts are real. False: pedestrian-only streets with bollards is an
   invented extrapolation.]_

## LEVEL 1: Believable (sounds totally plausible)

1. **Cart priority system** — Wider stepping stones at intersections meant your street
   had priority. Narrow stones = secondary road, yield to cross traffic. Primitive
   right-of-way signage.

2. **Night-shift thermopolia** — Some fast-food counters had oil lamps built into the
   counter, suggesting they operated at night. Night shifts were regulated by the aedile
   to prevent fire hazards.

3. **Phalluses as commercial good-luck charms** — The carved phalluses weren't pointing
   to the brothel. They were prosperity amulets — merchants carved them near their shops
   to attract good fortune. That's why there are so many on the Via dell'Abbondanza, the
   main commercial street. Like a horseshoe above a door.

4. **Facade colour codes** — The brightly painted walls indicated the type of business.
   Red = tavern, yellow = textile shop, blue = dye works. Changing your business type
   required repainting and paying a municipal fee.

5. **Mosaic census** — Floor mosaics at house entrances had to depict the number of
   inhabitants. It was a permanent visual census system.

---

## LEVEL 2: Dubious (sounds odd but who knows)

6. **Regulated graffiti** — Wall graffiti wasn't random vandalism. There were designated
   walls for public announcements and walls for personal messages. Writing on an
   unauthorized wall carried a fine. Romans had the distinction between a Facebook wall
   and a private WhatsApp group.

7. **Volcanic respiratory therapy** — Before the eruption, Pompeiians hiked to sulphur
   vents on Vesuvius because they believed the fumes cured lung diseases. Pliny mentions
   "the hot breath of the earth eases the lungs." It was common among the poor who
   couldn't afford a doctor.

8. **Cave Canem as status symbol** — The famous dog mosaic wasn't just a warning. An
   elaborate entrance mosaic was a wealth statement: the more detailed, the more
   expensive. The dog was popular because hunting dogs were status symbols. If you
   couldn't afford a real one, at least you had one on the floor.

9. **Amphitheatre betting tokens** — Hundreds of small terracotta tokens with numbers
   and gladiator names were found in tunnels under the amphitheatre. An organized
   betting system — buy a token before the fight, cash it in if your gladiator won.
   Romans invented the bookmaker.

10. **Pigeon postal service** — Pompeii used carrier pigeons to communicate with Naples
    and coastal villas. Numbered dovecote niches were found in several patrician homes —
    each niche corresponded to a different destination.

## LEVEL 3: Unlikely (raises eyebrows but delivered with confidence it works)

11. **Gladiator gym mirrors** — The amphitheatre training area had polished bronze plates
    on the walls so gladiators could check their posture and technique. Fragments with
    polishing marks were found in 1812 excavations but catalogued as "decorative
    elements" because nobody imagined Romans trained that way.

12. **Patrician escape tunnels** — The wealthiest villas in the north quarter had
    underground tunnels connecting to the port. Not sewers — private escape passages
    in case of slave revolts or invasion. Two have been found; only one is accessible
    and it's closed to the public.

13. **Geothermal bath heating** — Pompeii's baths used the standard hypocaust
    underfloor system, but some also tapped natural geothermal heat from Vesuvius.
    Certain bath water arrived pre-heated from underground. Romans used the volcano
    as a natural boiler.

14. **Wine pipeline** — A secondary terracotta pipe, smaller than the main aqueduct,
    carried unfermented grape juice from Vesuvius vineyards to city-centre cellars.
    Discovered in the 1950s, but only a 40-metre stretch survives, so archaeologists
    still argue about its actual function.

15. **The Vesuvius oracle** — A small temple near the Herculaneum Gate had a priestess
    who interpreted the volcano's tremors and smoke. Pompeiians consulted her before
    major decisions. Supposedly she warned weeks before the eruption that the mountain
    was "angry," but the town council ignored her to avoid panic.

## LEVEL 4: Very dubious (a stretch but still entertaining)

16. **Volcanic ash toothpaste** — Pompeiians mixed volcanic ash with urine as a tooth
    whitener. The urine part is true — Romans used it for bleaching. But Vesuvius ash
    was supposedly prized for its finer abrasive quality. Pompeii's dentists (yes, they
    existed) sold it in sealed jars as a premium product.

17. **The Cave Canem owner survived** — The owner of the House of the Tragic Poet
    wasn't in Pompeii on eruption day — he was in Rome on business. A fiscal record in
    the Herculaneum archives shows he reclaimed his properties a year later. His
    descendants supposedly lived in Naples until the 4th century.

18. **The first Roman sorbet** — Wealthy Pompeiians sent slaves to collect snow from
    Vesuvius's summit in winter, stored it in deep cellars under layers of straw, and
    mixed it with honey, fruit juice, and wine. Not ice cream exactly, but the same
    principle. Vesuvius was their freezer.

19. **The prophetic graffito** — On a wall near the forum there's graffiti that
    translates to: "This city will die by fire from the sky if it does not repent."
    Official guides say it's a common Christian graffito of the era. But dating
    suggests it was written just months before the eruption. Coincidence, probably.
    But it gives you chills.

20. **Coloured-stone voting** — In Pompeii's municipal elections, citizens didn't write
    on ballots. They dropped coloured stones into marble urns: red for one candidate,
    blue for another. Urns with stone residue were found in the forum. The oldest
    secret ballot system — because nobody could see what colour you dropped in.
